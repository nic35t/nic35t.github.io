# A new form of money at global scale

Projected annual stablecoin transfer volume has reached the tens of trillions of dollars. That scale alone tells us stablecoins are no longer a niche experiment. They are becoming part of how value moves through the digital economy.

So what exactly are stablecoins, and why do they matter?

### The three biggest use cases

Based on the data discussed in the original Korean post, stablecoin usage can be grouped into three major buckets.

1. **Crypto trading, roughly two-thirds of usage.** On global exchanges, stablecoins often function as the easiest practical proxy for dollars.
2. **Trade and remittances.** Stablecoins allow money to move across borders around the clock, often with less friction than legacy systems.
3. **Inflation hedging.** In countries with unstable local currencies, dollar-linked stablecoins can serve as a store of value.

A smaller but growing share is also being used directly in **payments**.

### Why this matters beyond crypto

The important point is that stablecoins are not defined only by speculation. In many cases, they are already acting as **digital representatives of fiat currency**. They sit at the intersection of finance, software, payments, and international money movement.

### The question that follows

If digital money is becoming a permanent layer of the financial system, then each country has to ask where its own currency stands in that transition.

For Korea, that naturally leads to the next question: **where does the Korean won fit inside this fast-growing market?**
