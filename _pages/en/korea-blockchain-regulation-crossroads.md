### A market with real strength, but a weak policy frame

The global digital-asset market is being reshaped quickly. Europe has MiCA. The United States, Hong Kong, and Singapore are all moving to strengthen domestic competitiveness inside regulated frameworks.

South Korea should be part of that front group. KRW already ranks behind only the U.S. dollar and euro in crypto trading volume, which means the country clearly has demand, liquidity, and market energy. The problem is that regulation has not turned those strengths into strategic advantage.

### The four barriers holding Korea back

Expert discussions around the Korean market point to four recurring obstacles.

1. **A Korean penalty for global firms.** International players often need to build an expensive Korea-only compliance and product stack because local rules diverge from global norms.
2. **Institutions are still stuck on the bench.** Without real participation from corporations and financial institutions, the market continues to look retail-heavy and immature.
3. **A half-built RWA market.** Rules that block tokenization of core assets such as treasuries or MMFs, while steering activity toward private chains only, make the market structurally limited.
4. **Regulation that fails to learn from past failures.** Korea has already seen what happens when overprotection suffocates an industry instead of guiding it.

### The real debate: control or growth?

At the center is a policy philosophy question.

One path tries to eliminate risk in advance through tight control. That may look safer on paper, but it often pushes talent, capital, and experimentation offshore. Crypto capital does not disappear when a country blocks it. It relocates.

The other path focuses on **growth inside a controlled framework**. That means using technology and standards to manage risks while still allowing the domestic industry to scale, compete, and create national advantage.

### What matters now

Korea already has several ingredients many countries would envy: a large active market, strong digital infrastructure, and a proven ability to execute policy shifts quickly when there is political will.

The core technical barriers are increasingly solvable. What remains is a policy decision: whether Korea wants to govern digital assets as a future industry or keep treating them mainly as a threat to be contained.
