### Diagnosis is not enough

Once you accept that Korea's digital-asset market is being held back by fragmentation and regulatory isolation, the next question is practical: what should be fixed first?

Among many possible reforms, one issue sits above the rest: **institutional participation**.

### Why institutions come first

There are at least three reasons this matters.

1. **Market maturity depends on it.** A market dominated almost entirely by retail flow is easily dismissed as speculative. Institutional capital brings depth, discipline, and a very different risk profile.
2. **RWA products require institutional rails.** Tokenized treasuries, MMFs, and similar real-world assets are not side projects. They are core products for serious capital pools, which means institutions must be allowed in for the market to matter.
3. **Global capital needs clear rules.** International firms may be interested in Korea, but they will not deploy major balance-sheet capital into a market where corporate and financial participation remains ambiguous.

### The master key effect

Korea has many separate policy bottlenecks: ICO limits, private-chain assumptions, uncertainty around tokenized assets, and more. But restrictions on institutions sit above them all.

If regulators clearly define how corporations and financial institutions can participate in digital assets, that single decision would do more than remove one obstacle. It would signal that the government sees digital assets as a strategic industry rather than a fringe speculation zone.

### What this unlocks

That signal changes the market psychology immediately. It improves capital formation, makes RWA more credible, and gives product builders a clearer sense of what kind of market they are actually building for.

In that sense, letting institutions in is not just another reform item. It is the **first move that makes the rest of the strategy possible**.
