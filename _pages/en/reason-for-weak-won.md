# Stability came with a cost

Why is the won less influential internationally than South Korea's economic scale might suggest?

The Korean article argues that the answer is not accidental. It is rooted in a policy tradeoff: Korea prioritized **stability** over **internationalization**.

### Stability versus openness

Policies designed to limit capital flight and preserve macroeconomic stability served a real purpose, especially in the shadow of past financial crises. They were not irrational rules.

But currencies gain global influence by moving through networks. If a currency cannot circulate freely enough, it becomes harder for it to grow into a widely used international instrument.

### The unintended result

Korea effectively protected the won from some external shocks while also limiting its ability to build international presence. That is the tradeoff in plain language.

The won did not become weak internationally because the economy lacked strength. It remained limited because policy placed a higher value on control and resilience than on global currency expansion.

### Why this matters now

Understanding this tradeoff is important for the stablecoin debate. A KRW stablecoin is not just a payments product. It is also part of a larger question about whether Korea wants the won to remain mostly domestic or gain a new route into global circulation.
