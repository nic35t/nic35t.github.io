# The business model is not as mysterious as it looks

One natural question about stablecoins is simple: if issuers give users convenience, incentives, or discounts, where does the profit come from?

The answer is the **reserve pool**.

### How the reserve model works

When users deposit fiat and receive stablecoins in return, the issuer holds the underlying cash reserves. Those reserves can then be invested in extremely safe assets such as short-dated government bonds.

That means the issuer's core revenue often comes from **managing reserves and earning low-risk yield**.

### A simple example

The Korean post uses a clear comparison: imagine a company gives customers a 2% discount for using its stablecoin-based payment flow, while the reserve assets earn 2.4% annually in safe government debt.

- **Cost:** 2% customer benefit
- **Income:** 2.4% reserve yield

That spread may look small, but at scale it becomes meaningful. The business can subsidize adoption and still remain economically viable.

### The important caution

This does not mean every stablecoin is a money-printing machine. The model works only if the reserve assets, regulations, liquidity structure, and financial plumbing are all built correctly.

Still, the underlying point matters: stablecoin issuance is not just a branding exercise. It can be a real business model when reserve management is designed well.
