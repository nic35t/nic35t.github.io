# Why AI business plans feel generic

Many people ask AI to "write a business plan for my idea" and expect a crisp strategy document back. What they often get instead is a polished but empty draft: generic claims, vague market logic, and sometimes outright hallucinations.

That is not mainly an AI problem. It is a context problem. If the model has not been fed the founder's actual assumptions, tradeoffs, and constraints, it fills the gaps with average-looking language. You are asking it to cook without ingredients.

This is where a **Havruta-style planning method** becomes useful.

---

# The core idea: AI should be an interviewer, not a ghostwriter

In the Jewish learning tradition of **Havruta**, people learn by asking, answering, and sharpening each other through dialogue. The same principle works for planning.

Instead of treating AI like a copywriter that should instantly generate the finished business plan, use it as a **disciplined interviewer** that helps surface the logic already inside your head.

You can think of this as a **prompt harness**. By forcing the model to stay inside the boundary of your answers, you reduce generic output and keep the draft grounded in real business thinking.

---

# A four-step workflow

### 1. Give the model an expert persona and a constraint

Do not start with a vague request. Put the model in the role of a sharp reviewer and make honesty mandatory.

> **Prompt:** "You are a cold-eyed VC investor and strategy planner. Review my idea critically. If something is unclear, do not invent facts. Ask me follow-up questions first."

### 2. Tell the AI to interview you first

Do not dump the idea and ask for the answer. Ask the model to identify what it needs to know.

> **Prompt:** "Before writing anything, ask me the 10 most important questions you need answered to judge whether this business can work."

### 3. Load context through Q&A

As you answer those questions, the model starts learning your logic: target users, unit economics, constraints, go-to-market choices, and the hidden assumptions you may not have noticed yourself.

The more concrete your answers are, the more useful the eventual output becomes. This is usually the moment when founders discover gaps in their own thinking.

### 4. Ask for the draft after the context is built

Once the dialogue contains enough real material, ask for the first draft.

> **Prompt:** "Based on everything we discussed, draft the first version of the business plan. Include the specific solutions, target metrics, and assumptions we already clarified."

---

# The real payoff

The value of this method is **control**. You are no longer dragged around by a model that defaults to average internet language. You are using AI to structure your own reasoning.

At first, this process can feel slower than simply saying "write it for me." In practice, it produces something far better: a plan with **your logic**, not a generic template wearing your company name.

If you want less fluff and more signal, stop asking AI to write first. Ask it to **question you first**.
